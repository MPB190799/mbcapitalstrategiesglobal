Understanding MLP Tax Implications: A Practical Guide for Income Investors

Master Limited Partnerships offer some of the highest yields in the market, but their tax treatment confuses many investors — and even some financial advisors. MLPs issue K-1 forms instead of 1099s, distributions are largely classified as return of capital (reducing your cost basis rather than creating current tax liability), and holding them in an IRA can trigger Unrelated Business Taxable Income above the $1,000 threshold. This guide walks through every scenario: taxable accounts, IRAs, state filing obligations, and what happens when you eventually sell. Mining · Feb 25, 2026

Gold Royalty Companies: The Lowest-Risk Way to Own the Gold Bull Market

Gold miners carry operational risk — cost overruns, permitting delays, geopolitical exposure. Royalty and streaming companies like Franco-Nevada, Wheaton Precious Metals, and Royal Gold sidestep most of these risks entirely. They provide upfront capital to miners in exchange for a percentage of future production at a predetermined price. The result: high margins (often above 75%), no exposure to AISC inflation, and organic growth as partner mines expand. With gold above $2,800/oz, these companies are compounding free cashflow and raising dividends. We analyze which royalty companies offer the best combination of yield, growth, and valuation today.

Strategy · Feb 18, 2026

The 80/20 Portfolio: How to Balance Yield and Growth in Hard Assets

Most income portfolios fail for one of two reasons: they chase yield into unsustainable payouts, or they play it so safe they underperform inflation. The 80/20 framework solves both problems. Allocate 80% to stable, well-covered distributions from pipelines, infrastructure MLPs, and blue-chip BDCs. These form your income foundation — boring, predictable, and growing at 3-5% annually. Then allocate 20% to high-upside cyclical plays: tanker stocks at the bottom of the cycle, junior miners with exploration catalysts, or beaten-down commodity producers. This 20% sleeve provides the asymmetric returns that turbocharge total performance while the 80% sleeve pays the bills.

Commodity Supercycles Explained: Are We in One Right Now?

Commodity supercycles are multi-decade periods of structurally rising real commodity prices driven by demand shocks that outpace supply response. The last supercycle ran roughly from 2000 to 2011, fueled by Chinese industrialization. Today, a confluence of forces — the energy transition demanding vast quantities of copper, lithium, and rare earths; years of underinvestment in oil and gas exploration; reshoring of manufacturing; and aging mine supply — suggests we may be in the early innings of a new supercycle. We examine the historical evidence, compare current conditions to prior cycles, and discuss how income investors should position across energy, metals, and agricultural commodities.
